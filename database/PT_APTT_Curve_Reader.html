Section 1
The Three Optical Signals
The analyzer records the raw photodetector signal and computes two derived curves in real time. All three are plotted together and used jointly to determine and cross-validate the clot time.
Original reaction curve — Luminous Flux
Luminous flux through sample — photodiode detector (405 / 545 / 660 nm tri-color LED)

The raw luminous flux detected by the photodiode as light passes through the plasma sample. Per the IFU (Section 4.5), the analyzer uses a tri-color LED (405 nm, 545 nm, 660 nm) and automatically selects the most suitable wavelength based on assay type and sample interference level. Flux is high when plasma is clear and decreases as fibrin clot forms and turbidity increases.

IFU Y-axis label: Luminous Flux (raw photodiode units)  ·  Direction: Decreasing  ·  IFU terms: Start Point (beginning) → Clot Point → End Point (end)
1. Deriv. — First derivative reaction curve
Rate of change of luminous flux at each instant

The instantaneous rate of change of luminous flux. Values are negative throughout clotting because flux is falling. The most negative point — the peak — marks the moment of fastest fibrin polymerization. The CX-6010 calls this the Clot Point, which is the reported clot time in seconds.

IFU name: “1. Deriv.”  ·  Peak: most negative value = Clot Point  ·  Depth: reflects clotting speed and fibrinogen level
2. Deriv. — Second derivative reaction curve
Rate of change of the first derivative

The acceleration or deceleration of clot formation. Negative before the Clot Point (clotting is speeding up), zero at the Clot Point, positive after (clotting is slowing down). The zero crossing independently confirms the Clot Point detected by 1. Deriv.

IFU name: “2. Deriv.”  ·  Zero crossing = Clot Point confirmation  ·  Before Clot Point: negative (accelerating)  ·  After: positive (decelerating)
Section 2
Curve Anatomy
The original reaction curve is divided into distinct zones. Each corresponds to a stage of coagulation. Understanding each zone helps identify where and why a reaction is abnormal.
Start Point (Baseline)

Stable flat region at the start of the reaction (IFU term: Start Point). Luminous flux is high and constant because the plasma is clear. The analyzer uses this zone to set the signal reference level and detect sample quality problems. An unstable or low flux at this stage is the first indicator of interference.

IFU term: Start Point  ·  Normal: Flux stable and high  ·  Low flux baseline: lipemia, hemolysis  ·  Unstable: micro-bubbles, temperature drift
Lag phase

Brief period after reagent activation where the coagulation cascade is propagating but fibrin polymerization has not yet begun. T starts to fall very slightly. PT has a shorter lag (tissue factor directly activates Factor VII→X); APTT has a longer lag (contact activation through XII→XI→IX).

PT lag: ~2–4 s  ·  APTT lag: ~5–10 s  ·  Extended lag may indicate reduced contact activation (Factor XII or XI deficiency)
Clotting phase — sigmoid drop

The rapid sigmoid descent where fibrinogen is actively polymerizing into fibrin. This phase produces the largest rate of luminous flux change — 1. Deriv. and 2. Deriv. are at their maximum magnitudes here. A steep rapid drop indicates robust coagulation; a gradual slope suggests factor deficiency, inhibitor, or low fibrinogen.

Steep slope: strong clotting activity  ·  Slow or gradual slope: factor deficiency, inhibitor, or low fibrinogen
Clot Point (Inflection point)

The midpoint of the sigmoid where the rate of flux change is at its absolute maximum (IFU term: Clot Point). This is simultaneously: (1) the peak of 1. Deriv., (2) the zero crossing of 2. Deriv., and (3) the reported clot time in seconds. The CX-6010 marks this with a vertical “Clot Point” line across all three curves.

IFU term: Clot Point  ·  Identified by: peak of |1.Deriv.| = zero crossing of 2.Deriv. = reported clot time (seconds)
End Point (Plateau)

Stable flat region at the end of the reaction when fibrin clot formation is complete (IFU term: End Point). Luminous flux is low and constant and both derivatives return to approximately zero. The End Point level determines the amplitude. A high End Point (flux barely decreased) indicates a weak or incomplete clot despite a detectable Clot Point.

IFU term: End Point  ·  Normal: Flux low and stable  ·  High End Point: incomplete clot  ·  Both 1.Deriv. and 2.Deriv. ≈ 0
Amplitude — ΔT
ΔFlux = Flux(Start Point) − Flux(End Point)

The difference in luminous flux from Start Point to End Point. Reflects the quality and completeness of fibrin clot formation. A result with a normal Clot Point but low amplitude means the fibrin clot formed is structurally weak. The blue curve (normal) shows a large flux drop; the red dashed curve (low fibrinogen) shows a small drop.

Normal: >70%  ·  Warning flag: <40%  ·  Critical: <20% → fibrinogen likely critically low  ·  Causes: fibrinogen <100 mg/dL, dysfibrinogenemia, elevated D-dimer
Section 3
Reported Values
Numerical values derived from the reaction curve and reported to the clinician. Understanding how each is calculated helps interpret unusual results correctly.
Clot time (seconds)
Time from Start Point to Clot Point (IFU: “coagulation end time”)

Time elapsed from reaction start (Start Point) to the Clot Point, identified by the peak of the 1. Deriv. curve. The IFU (Section 4.2) describes this as detecting the “coagulation end time.” Reported in seconds and compared against a laboratory reference interval. Results outside the reference require investigation of the affected pathway.

PT reference: ~10–14 s (reagent-dependent)  ·  APTT reference: ~25–35 s (reagent-sensitive)  ·  Reference intervals must be validated locally for each reagent lot
INR — International Normalized Ratio
INR = (Patient PT ÷ Mean Normal PT)ISI

Standardizes PT across different reagent–analyzer combinations worldwide. The ISI (International Sensitivity Index) is unique to each thromboplastin reagent lot and corrects for sensitivity differences. INR makes warfarin monitoring consistent between laboratories and countries.

Therapeutic: 2.0–3.0 most indications  ·  2.5–3.5 mechanical heart valves  ·  Not valid for liver disease, acute coagulopathy, or non-warfarin patients
APTT Ratio
Ratio = Patient APTT ÷ Mean Normal APTT

Expresses the patient APTT relative to the local mean normal value. Used for monitoring unfractionated heparin (UFH) therapy. Unlike INR, the APTT ratio is not internationally standardized — each laboratory must derive its own therapeutic range by correlating APTT ratio against plasma anti-Xa activity.

UFH therapeutic: ratio ~1.5–2.5 (must be locally validated)  ·  Not for LMWH — use anti-Xa assay instead  ·  Elevated ratio without heparin → inhibitor or severe deficiency
Amplitude (%)
ΔFlux % = Flux(Start Point) − Flux(End Point), relative to total range

The relative change in luminous flux from Start Point to End Point, expressed as a percentage of the full detection range. Represents the structural quality of the fibrin clot — how completely fibrinogen was converted to fibrin. A normal Clot Point with low amplitude means the clot formed is weak and the result may be unreliable. Always correlate with Clauss fibrinogen.

Normal: >70%  ·  Warning flag: <40%  ·  Critical: <20% → run Clauss fibrinogen urgently  ·  Causes: fibrinogen <100 mg/dL, dysfibrinogenemia, DIC, elevated D-dimer
Section 4
Signal Quality and Interference
Conditions that distort the reaction curve and compromise the reliability of results. Always review the curve shape alongside the reported number. A number without a curve is incomplete information.
Noise and fluctuation

Random optical spikes superimposed on the true signal. Sources include photon shot noise, temperature micro-fluctuations, cuvette surface scatter, and micro-bubbles. The analyzer applies a smoothing algorithm before computing derivatives to prevent noise from creating false peaks. Heavy noise makes the first derivative unreliable.

Causes: lipemia, air bubble injection, dirty optics, temperature instability  ·  Heavy noise: derivatives invalid → flag or reject result
HIL interference (Hemolysis · Icterus · Lipemia)

H, I, and L all shift the optical baseline by absorbing or scattering light independently of fibrin formation. This distorts both the starting flux level and the amplitude, producing unreliable clot times. The CX-6010 uses its tri-color LED (405/545/660 nm) to automatically detect interference levels and selects the most suitable wavelength or flags affected results (HIL 9×9 Grid).

H hemolysis: raises absorbance, lowers T  ·  I icterus: bilirubin absorbs specific wavelengths  ·  L lipemia: fat particles scatter light severely (most disruptive)
Low amplitude pattern

A shallow flux drop indicating insufficient fibrin formation. The Clot Point time may appear normal, but the fibrin clot is structurally weak. The 1. Deriv. peak will be low in magnitude and the End Point will be high relative to the Start Point flux. Can produce a falsely reported Clot Point if amplitude is critically low.

Flag threshold: ΔT <40%  ·  Action: run Clauss fibrinogen  ·  Causes: fibrinogen <100 mg/dL, dysfibrinogenemia, DIC, elevated FDP/D-dimer, uremia
No reaction — ERROR pattern

Luminous flux stays flat and high after reagent addition — no clot forms. Both 1. Deriv. and 2. Deriv. remain near zero with no Clot Point detected. The analyzer reports an ERROR (IFU error code 48340XXX: “No clotting reaction is detected”). Most commonly caused by a pre-clotted tube, but also by severely depleted clotting factors or incorrect sample type.

Causes: pre-clotted tube  ·  factors consumed in DIC  ·  wrong tube type  ·  Action: REJECT — recollect in fresh 3.2% citrate, verify 1:9 fill ratio
Biphasic first derivative

Two distinct peaks in the 1. Deriv. curve, indicating clot formation occurred in two stages. Most commonly caused by high-dose or residual heparin, where partial anticoagulation allows an initial weak fibrin network followed by a secondary stronger event. Also seen with dysfibrinogenemia. The analyzer may report the larger second peak as the Clot Point or flag the result.

Causes: heparin contamination, residual thrombin inhibitor, dysfibrinogenemia  ·  Action: check heparin status; consider heparinase-treated repeat if available
Smoothing algorithm

Before computing derivatives, the analyzer applies a digital smoothing filter to the raw luminous flux signal. This removes high-frequency noise without distorting the underlying sigmoid shape. Without smoothing, noise spikes create multiple false peaks in 1. Deriv., making true Clot Point detection impossible.

Why it matters: without smoothing, noise spikes produce false peaks in 1. Deriv. — Clot Point cannot be reliably detected  ·  Smoothing preserves peak position while removing false peaks  ·  Applied internally by the CX-6010 before reporting
